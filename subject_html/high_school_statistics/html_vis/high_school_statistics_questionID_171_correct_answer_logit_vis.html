A convenience sample is one where participants are selected because they are easy to access. That might include friends, family, or people you meet, but it's not random. It's often biased because it doesn't represent the entire population. A simple random sample (SRS) is where every individual in the population has an equal chance of being selected. This is done by assigning each person a number and then randomly picking a sample. It's unbiased and gives each group an equal chance, but it might not account for different subgroups within the population. A stratified sample involves dividing the population into subgroups, or strata, based on certain characteristics. Then, a random sample is taken from each stratum. This method ensures that each subgroup is represented proportionally, which can lead to more accurate results, especially when different groups might have different attitudes. A systematic sample is where you select individuals from a population at regular intervals. For example, every 10th person on a list. Similarly, a systematic sample might inadvertently exclude certain groups if the list is ordered in a particular way. This way, the study can capture the attitudes of each subgroup accurately, leading to more reliable and valid results.
